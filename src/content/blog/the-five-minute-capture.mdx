You don’t need a longer quiet time.

You need a shorter <mark>landing</mark> for what the quiet time already produced.

Most “I can’t remember what I read” problems are capture problems. The thought was real. It just never got a place to sit. ([Why thoughts disappear](/blog/why-bible-study-thoughts-disappear) is the longer version of that diagnosis.)

Here’s a five-minute capture you can run before the day grabs you.

---

## The rule

If it takes more than five minutes, you won’t do it on a normal Tuesday.

So the capture is three lines, max:

1. **Reference** — where you were
2. **One sentence** — what you don’t want to lose
3. **One question** — what’s still open

That’s it. No journal entry. No application paragraph you don’t believe yet.

---

## Why three lines work

The sentence forces clarity. Vague feelings (“that was encouraging”) fade. A sentence you could text a friend tends to stick.

The question keeps you honest. Scripture often leaves you mid-thought. Writing the open edge is how tomorrow’s reading starts warmer.

The reference is the index. When the same passage shows up in a sermon three months later, you can find yourself again.

---

## Where to put it

Anywhere is better than nowhere. Connected to the passage is better than anywhere.

Phone notes apps are fast and forgetful. Margins are honest and unsearchable. A notes hub linked to Scripture is built for this exact job: <mark>capture now, find later</mark>.

Keep your Bible reader. Add a place for the three lines.

---

## When you skip a day

Skip without drama. The habit is resilient or it isn’t a habit.

Next time you sit down, don’t “catch up” five missed captures. Capture today. Continuity compounds from the days you show up, not from guilt math.

---

## Pair it with return

Once or twice a week, reopen last capture before you read something new. Thirty seconds. Ask: *Am I still carrying that sentence?*

That’s the personal version of a [Monday retention loop](/blog/the-monday-retention-loop)—smaller, quieter, still retrieval.
