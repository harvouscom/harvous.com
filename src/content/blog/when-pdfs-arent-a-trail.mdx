Churches are good at collecting PDFs.

Lesson plans. Series outlines. Kids packets. Small-group questions. A Drive folder named `Fall 2024 FINAL` next to one named `Fall 2024 FINAL v2`.

Those files can be useful. They are not the same thing as a <mark>trail</mark>.

A trail is what a new teacher can reopen and understand what the room has already walked through—passages, aims, questions that landed, loose ends still open. A PDF is often just last quarter’s delivery notes, frozen.

---

## Files keep content

Content is the outline you meant to say.

Continuity is what the people actually carried.

When the only handoff is a zip of worksheets, the next leader inherits paper, not memory. They rebuild the arc from scratch. The church quietly pays that tax every semester.

If you’ve felt [church education evaporate by Monday](/blog/why-church-education-evaporates-by-monday) inside one week, multiply that by a leadership change. The fade gets structural.

---

## What a trail includes

Keep this short enough that someone will actually write it down:

- The passage(s) for the unit
- One aim sentence (what you’re after, not a slogan)
- Two questions that opened the room
- One open thread for next time
- Where this sits in a longer series (week 3 of 8, after Easter, before the next book)

That’s enough for a human to continue. It’s not enough to become a ChMS feature checklist.

See [what church education systems need](/blog/what-church-education-systems-need) for the wider principles.

---

## Where trails should live

Trails fail when they only live in one person’s laptop.

They also fail when “the system” means a portal nobody opens except the admin.

Prefer a place teachers already return to for prep—notes tied to Scripture, not another login for PDFs. Keep your Bible app. Keep Planning Center for people and rooms. Add a working library for the learning itself.

Shared Spaces and church-wide curriculum tools are still on the road for Harvous. The principle doesn’t wait on the feature: <mark>write the trail where teaching happens</mark>, not only where files are stored.

---

## A light staff habit

Once a month, ask three questions in a staff or teacher huddle:

1. What did we actually cover (passage + aim)?
2. What did people wrestle with?
3. What should next month’s leader already know?

Write the answers somewhere findable. Ten minutes. No new binder.

If you can’t answer those three, you don’t have a system yet—you have a content archive.
