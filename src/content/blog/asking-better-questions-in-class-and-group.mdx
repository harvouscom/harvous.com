A good question can open a passage.

A bad question can close a room.

Most of us learned questioning by imitation: the leader asks something with a clear “right” answer, waits 1.5 seconds, then answers it themselves. Or we swing the other way—questions so vague (“How did that make you feel?”) that the text becomes optional.

Better questions are a craft. You can practice them. You don’t need a personality transplant.

---

## What a study question is for

In class or group, a question should help people:

1. **Look** at the text again
2. **Think** with the text, not around it
3. **Speak** as humans in a room—honestly, kindly

If a question can be answered without the passage open, it’s probably not a study question. It might still be a pastoral question. Just know which one you’re asking.

---

## Three kinds that earn a place

### Observation — “What do you notice?”

Low shame. High return. Especially good for mixed rooms (new readers and long-time readers together).

Examples:

- What repeated words or ideas do you see in these verses?
- Who is speaking, and to whom?
- What surprises you if you read it slowly?

### Meaning — “What is this saying?”

This is where people fear being wrong. Make it safe by asking for *possible* readings and by returning to context.

Examples:

- Given what came before, what does this sentence seem to be doing?
- What would the first hearers have found comforting—or difficult?
- Where does this push back on a shallow reading of the chapter?

### Response — “What is ours to carry?”

Keep this proportionate. Not every text wants a five-point life plan by 8:45 p.m.

Examples:

- What is one honest response this week—without pretending we’re done with the passage?
- Who in your life comes to mind as you hear this—and how will you pray, not perform?
- What might obedience look like in ordinary hours, not only dramatic ones?

## Habits that improve questions

<mark>Write them before you improvise.</mark> Improvisation is fine; amnesia isn’t. Put two questions in your [lesson-prep stack](/blog/the-lesson-prep-stack) so the trail includes what you intended to ask.

**Prefer fewer.** Two strong questions beat eight mediocre ones. Dead air is not failure. It’s space.

**Ask the text to referee.** When answers diverge, smile and say: “Let’s look again at verse 7.” The Bible stays the center; you stay the guide.

**Catch the live ones.** The best question of the night often comes from someone in the third row. After class, add it to your trail note. That’s how your questioning craft <mark>compounds</mark>.

**Don’t use questions as traps.** If you already have a single acceptable sentence in mind, consider teaching that sentence plainly instead of playing “guess what I’m thinking.”

---

## What to avoid (gently)

- Rapid-fire quiz questions that reward the quickest talker
- Questions that demand vulnerable disclosure from people who just met
- Questions that smuggle your sermon back in as “discussion”
- Questions that treat doubt as a problem to eliminate in ten minutes

Curiosity and care can sit together. I’m not saying Psalm 139:23–24 is a small-group method—it’s a prayer of honest searching before God. (See [Psalm 139:23–24](https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Psalm+139%3A23-24&version=NIV).) The tone still helps: invite real looking without turning anyone’s questions into a spectacle. Your group isn’t a stage; it’s a place where searching is allowed.

---

## A practice for four weeks

Each week, prepare:

- 1 observation question
- 1 meaning question
- 0–1 response question

Afterward, rate them privately: Which one actually sent people back to the page? Keep that kind. Retire the ones that only produced polite fog.

If you teach or lead regularly, keep those questions next to the passage so next quarter’s you can learn from this quarter’s room. [Teachers](/for/teachers/) and [group leaders](/for/group-leaders/) both need that memory.

Better questions won’t make you impressive. They’ll make the text more audible—and the people more present.
