Most people highlight like they’re decorating.

Yellow on a verse. Maybe a second color for “really important.” Three months later they can’t name a single line they marked—and the app’s highlight list is a graveyard of good intentions.

A highlight only counts if you can <mark>find it again on purpose</mark>.

---

## What goes wrong

Bible-app highlights are easy to make and hard to reuse. They’re stuck inside a reader that isn’t where you prep, teach, or journal.

Phone screenshots of verses are worse: unsearchable, unlinked, and somehow still all over your camera roll.

---

## A better highlight habit

In Harvous, treat a highlight as a seed for a note—not a substitute for one.

1. Mark the line that actually stopped you
2. Add one sentence: why it stopped you
3. Keep the scripture pill on it so the mark has an address

If you only have ten seconds, the mark + pill is enough. The sentence can come later the same day.

---

## Highlights and recall

Marks you never reopen are museum pieces.

Once a week, skim three old highlights and try to say the sentence without looking. That’s personal retrieval—the same idea behind the [Monday retention loop](/blog/the-monday-retention-loop), shrunk to a coffee line.

---

## Keep your reader

You don’t have to abandon the Bible app you like.

Keep reading there. When a line matters, give it a home in your notes hub—with the reference attached—so the highlight isn’t trapped in a reader you’ll swipe past tomorrow.

That’s the job: <mark>marks that stay useful</mark>, not marks that look devout.
